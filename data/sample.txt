Banking fundamentals explain how money moves through an economy.
A bank accepts deposits from customers and keeps those deposits safe.
A savings account helps people store money and earn interest over time.
A checking account makes it easier to pay bills and manage daily spending.
A loan allows a customer to borrow money and repay it in scheduled installments.
Interest is the cost of borrowing money and the reward for saving money.
Credit risk describes the chance that a borrower may fail to repay a loan.
Banks study income, collateral, and repayment history before approving credit.
A mortgage is a long term loan used to buy a home.
A business loan helps a company invest in equipment, inventory, or expansion.
Liquidity means a bank can meet withdrawal requests when customers need cash.
Capital acts as a financial cushion that helps a bank absorb losses.
The central bank influences interest rates and supports financial stability.
Higher policy rates can slow borrowing, while lower rates can encourage lending.
Inflation reduces purchasing power when prices rise too quickly.
Regulation helps banks protect depositors, control risk, and follow the law.
Fraud detection systems monitor unusual transactions and flag suspicious behavior.
Customer trust is essential because banking depends on confidence and reliability.
Digital banking allows customers to transfer funds, review balances, and pay online.
Risk management helps a bank balance growth, safety, and profitability.
